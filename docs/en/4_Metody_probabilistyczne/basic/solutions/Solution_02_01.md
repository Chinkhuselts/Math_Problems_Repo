### Task 1 — Recognizing Counting Models

**1. Arranging 7 students in a line.**
* **Model:** Permutation
* **Reasoning:** We are using *all* 7 objects (students), order matters (a line implies positions like 1st, 2nd, 3rd), there are no identical students, and it is a linear arrangement.

**2. Choosing 4 members of a committee from 12 people.**
* **Model:** Combination
* **Reasoning:** We are only using *some* of the objects (4 out of 12), and the order in which we select the committee members does not matter. Repetition is not allowed since a person cannot be chosen twice.

**3. Assigning gold, silver, and bronze medals among 15 athletes.**
* **Model:** k-permutation (ordered selection without repetition)
* **Reasoning:** We are using *some* of the objects (3 out of 15). Order matters because getting Gold is different from getting Bronze. Repetition is not allowed.

**4. Forming a 6-digit PIN code.**
* **Model:** Sequence with repetition
* **Reasoning:** We are choosing a specific number of items (6 digits from the 10 possible digits 0-9). Order matters, and repetition is allowed (e.g., a PIN can be 000000). 

**5. Arranging the letters of the word BANANA.**
* **Model:** Permutation with repeated elements
* **Reasoning:** We are arranging *all* the objects (all 6 letters), and order matters. However, some objects are identical (three 'A's and two 'N's), so we must use the model for repeated elements to avoid overcounting.

**6. Seating 6 people around a round table.**
* **Model:** Circular permutation
* **Reasoning:** We are arranging *all* the objects (6 people), but they are arranged in a continuous circle. Only their relative positions to one another matter.
